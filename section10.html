Chapter 7 point 2: Wisely Employ Leverage (Margin)

Used properly, margin is one of the most valuable tools in a trader’s arsenal. Most traders are familiar with Regular Margin or Reg-T margin however, In this chapter, we will explore the advantages of employing Portfolio Margin and/or SPAN margin to better weather market events, diversify your portfolio, and potentially yield greater returns.

Understanding SPAN and Portfolio Margin

SPAN and Portfolio Margin are risk-based margin calculation methods that differ from the traditional Reg-T margin used by most brokers. While Reg-T margin calculates the margin requirement based on the full risk of the trade at expiration, SPAN and Portfolio Margin take a more nuanced approach.

These advanced margin types consider factors such as the probability of the trade reaching certain profit and loss levels, the time to expiration, and the overall risk of the portfolio. By taking a more holistic view of your positions, SPAN and Portfolio Margin can often result in lower margin requirements, allowing you to make better use of your trading capital.

The Types of Margin

  1. Reg-T Margin (Regular Margin):

    Reg-T margin is the standard margin used by most brokers. It provides 2:1 leverage, meaning that for every $1 in your account, you can control $2 worth of positions. However, the Reg-T margin calculates the margin requirement based on the full risk of the trade at expiration, which can limit your buying power and potential returns.

  2. Portfolio Margin:

    Portfolio Margin is a risk-based margin calculation that assesses the overall risk of your portfolio, rather than looking at each position in isolation. This approach can lead to lower margin requirements, allowing you to maximize your buying power and potentially increase your returns. To qualify for Portfolio Margin, you typically need a minimum account balance of $100,000 and to pass a broker-specific eligibility test.

  3. SPAN Margin:

    SPAN Margin - which is an acronym for Standard Portfolio Analysis of Risk - is another risk-based margin calculation method specifically designed for futures and options on futures. Like Portfolio Margin, SPAN Margin evaluates the overall risk of your portfolio and can result in lower margin requirements compared to Reg-T margin. SPAN Margin is typically available to traders with capital of $2,000 or more, making it an attractive option for those trading futures options.

Advantage number 1 of SPAN/Portfolio Margin: Early Management, High Return Optimization

One of the most significant advantages of using SPAN or Portfolio Margin is the potential for higher returns on your capital/margin compared to traditional Reg-T margin. By allowing you to utilize more of your capital for trading, these margin types enable you to optimize your returns through early management of your positions.

To illustrate the return on capital advantage, let’s consider a hypothetical example based on the margin calculation we highlighted in the previous section for an unbalanced Iron Condor.

Margin or Buying Power UsedProfit Target% Return
Regular Margin$12,000$1,0008.33%
Portfolio or SPAN Margin$5,000$1,00020.00%

In this example, a trader using Reg-T margin would need to allocate $12,000 in buying power to enter a trade with a profit target of $1,000. If the trade reaches its profit target, the trader would realize an 8.33% return on the margin used.

However, a trader using SPAN or Portfolio Margin might only need to allocate $5,000 in buying power for the same trade, due to the more favorable margin calculations. If the trade reaches its $1,000 profit target, the trader will achieve a significantly higher return of 20.00% on the margin used.

The lower margin requirements of SPAN/Portfolio Margin allow you to manage your trades more efficiently. With less capital tied up in each trade, you can close out positions when they reach their profit targets early rather than waiting until expiration and make more efficient use of capital.

This early management approach can be particularly beneficial in situations where the market moves in your favor early on, allowing you to lock in profits and free up capital for other opportunities. By actively managing your positions before they reach expiration, you can optimize your returns and make more efficient use of your trading capital.

Advantage Number 2 of SPAN/Portfolio Margin: Increased Flexibility in Trade Selection

The increased buying power afforded by SPAN and Portfolio Margin also allows you to be more flexible in which option trade structure you can employ. With more capital at your disposal, you can consider a wider range of strategies and take advantage of opportunities that may have been out of reach with Reg-T margin.

For example, you may be able to take on more complex, multi-leg options strategies that require higher margin requirements, such as unsymmetrical options strategies. These strategies can offer unique risk-reward profiles and help you navigate various market conditions more effectively.

The increased flexibility in trade selection, combined with the potential for early management and higher returns on capital, makes SPAN and Portfolio Margin powerful tools for options traders looking to optimize their performance and adapt to changing market conditions.

Advantage Number 3 of SPAN/Portfolio Margin: Profit Stacking

Another significant advantage of using SPAN or Portfolio Margin is the ability to “double dip” or “profit stack.” This means you can effectively use your buying power to trade an efficient options structure on top of going long on other assets, such as bonds or stocks.

For example, if you had $100,000 in starting capital, you could buy $100,000 worth of treasury bonds yielding 1-4% or more per year and still have around $99,000 available to trade options. This essentially allows you to have $100,000 in your account while trading with $200,000, effectively doubling your buying power.

When it comes to profit stacking with equities, according to Charles Schwab, when using Portfolio or SPAN margin, it is possible to use 100% of your capital to go long stocks or indices (e.g., SPY) and then use an additional 85% to trade options.

However, it’s important to note that margin levels and requirements can change with fluctuations in stock prices. For example, if you had an account size of $100,000 and used 85% of the capital to trade options, a 10% drop in SPY would result in a net liquidation value of $90,000. In this case, 85% of $90,000 would be $76,500 available for options trading.

To mitigate the risk of margin calls due to fluctuations in stock prices, it’s advisable to use a more conservative approach when profit stacking with equities. A good rule of thumb is to use no more than 50% of your planned capital to trade options when going long stocks simultaneously.

Potential Risks and Considerations

While the advantages of SPAN and Portfolio Margin are significant, it’s essential to understand the potential risks and considerations associated with these margin types.

  1. Increased Leverage and Risk: The increased buying power offered by SPAN and Portfolio Margin also comes with increased leverage and potential risk. If your positions move against you, the losses can be amplified and you may face the risk of a margin call or forced liquidation.
  2. Margin Expansion: As market conditions change, the margin requirements for your positions may increase, a phenomenon known as margin expansion. This can lead to a reduction in your buying power and potentially force you to close positions or add more capital to your account to meet margin requirements.
  3. Complexity and Monitoring: Using SPAN or Portfolio Margin requires a deeper understanding of margin calculations and risk management. You’ll need to closely monitor your positions, margin requirements, and account balance to ensure that you’re not overexposed to risk.

Conclusion

SPAN and Portfolio Margin offer options traders several key advantages, including the potential for higher returns on capital using early management, increased flexibility in trade selection, and the ability to profit stack by trading options alongside other assets.

By understanding and utilizing these advantages, options traders can optimize their performance, adapt to changing market conditions, and potentially increase their overall returns. However, it’s crucial to approach these margin types with caution and employ disciplined risk management and position sizing to navigate the increased leverage and potential risks effectively.
