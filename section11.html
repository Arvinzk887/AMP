Chapter 7 point 3: Crash-Proof Position Sizing

In the previous chapter, we explored why wisely employing SPAN/Portfolio margin can provide traders greater flexibility and potentially higher returns due to several key advantages, including profit stacking, increased flexibility in trade structures, and the concept of early management, for more efficient return optimization. We also covered potential risks and considerations with using margin including the concept of margin expansion.

In this chapter, we will delve into some guidelines for optimally sizing an options portfolio with the goal of appropriate risk management and efficient capital utilization.

What Is Position Sizing?

In layman’s terms, position sizing is basically how much buying power, planned capital, or margin you’re going to allocate to a trade or to your trading strategy as a whole taking into account that buying power requirements can change.

It is the process of determining the optimal number of contracts to trade based on your account size, risk tolerance, and the specific characteristics of the trade itself.

Why Size Matters

Proper position sizing helps to ensure that no single trade can decimate your account, while also allowing you to capitalize on profitable opportunities.

It doesn’t matter what trading style you have — whether you are buying options or selling options, following a trend, or trading non-directionally — position sizing is important for all of it. However, in the case of option selling using margin, it is even more critical because of the associated tail risk and the effects that volatility expansion events and dynamic margin requirements can have on a portfolio.

One should never risk blowing up their entire account due to improper position sizing.

Common Approaches to Position Sizing

Position sizing is a critical aspect of options trading that is often not fully understood by both new and experienced traders alike because it is one of the most challenging aspects of optimizing a trading plan.

One common approach to position sizing is the fixed percentage method. With this approach, traders risk a fixed percentage of their account balance on each trade. For example, a trader might risk 2 percent of their account on every trade. If the account balance is fifyt-thousand dollars, the trader would allocate thousand dollars to each position. This method helps to ensure that no single trade can significantly deplete the account, as the risk is spread across multiple positions.

Another approach is to dedicate a specific portion of buying power to a particular strategy. For example, a trader may choose to allocate only 30 percent of their buying power to a single strategy to manage risk.

The All-Market Profits Approach

I take a more nuanced approach to position sizing that accounts for the unique aspects of a trade structure and the overall market environment. Our primary objective is to create a crash-proof portfolio that can withstand even the most severe market downturns while still allowing for optimal capital efficiency and returns. To achieve this, we must account for volatility expansion, margin expansion, worst-case scenario analysis, and maximum margin requirements.

Key Position Sizing Considerations: Volatility Expansion & Margin Expansion

Volatility expansion typically occurs during binary events or violent selloffs, causing existing positions to use more of our available buying power, even if the strikes are not close to being in-the-money. This is a significant risk for option sellers, as it can lead to margin calls and forced liquidations. Margin expansion occurs when the amount of margin required to maintain an open position increases as time passes.

Margin expansion can occur in two different scenarios: when selling naked options or when using SPAN and/or Portfolio Margin. It happens when the amount of margin required to maintain an open position increases as time passes, even if the broker initially approved the trade. This happens because the broker’s initial assessment of the required margin may no longer be sufficient as market conditions change.

For example, let’s say you open a position with an initial margin requirement of twleve thousand dollars, and your account has a balance of fifteen thousand dollars. Your broker approves the trade, and you have three thousand dollars in available funds. However, as the trade progresses, you may see your available balance decrease to seven thousand dollars, while your margin requirement expands to sixteen thousand dollars. This situation puts you at risk of over-leveraging your account and potentially facing a margin call.

Maximum Margin Requirement Through Worst-Case Scenario Analysis

Maximum Margin Requirement is the highest amount of margin needed to maintain an open position under the most adverse market conditions. It is determined by thoroughly backtesting a trading strategy across various market scenarios, including normal market conditions and worst-case situations like market crash periods of high volatility or depending on the trade structure a low volatility environment.

By evaluating these scenarios during backtesting, traders can identify the highest margin requirement encountered, which becomes the Maximum Margin Requirement.

Once you have determined the Maximum Margin Requirement and understand the potential for margin expansion, you can use this information to make informed decisions about position sizing. I’ve listed below my process for determining Position Sizing.

My Process for Determining Optimal Position Sizing

When it comes to determining optimal position sizing, I follow a thorough and systematic approach that involves both art and science. Again, my goal is to have no surprises when it comes to drawdowns. This is especially important in an options portfolio where leverage is involved. I look at actual drawdowns versus actual returns when I calculate the worstcase scenario. This process allows me to properly size my positions within the portfolio.

Below is an overview of the steps I take for determining position sizing:

Step number 1) Backtest One Tranche of the Trading Structure

The first step I take when determining the optimal position sizing and planned capital to devote to a trade is to backtest one tranche of the trading structure that I want to analyze. I do this over a significant period, typically 8 years or more, which includes various market conditions. By focusing on backtesting a single tranche, I can gain a clear understanding of how the trade structure performs and what the margin requirements might look like in different scenarios.

Step number 2) Analyze Margin Usage

After backtesting one tranche of the trade structure, I examine the margin being used by the trade structure for each day within the backtest period. This is a meticulous process where I look at every single day from the backtested period, identifying the day with the highest margin usage. This day represents the worst-case scenario, and the highest margin requirement encountered during this backtested period is what I refer to as the Maximum Margin Requirement.

Step number 3) Establish a Maximum Margin Requirement Buffer

Once I have determined the Maximum Margin Requirement, I take into account the fact that potential future market conditions may be worse than the historical worst-case scenario. To ensure that I have sufficient capital to withstand worstcase scenarios without risking a margin call, I set my position sizing so that the worst-case margin expansion remains under a certain buffer based on my targeted risk profile for the trade. For example, 80% maximum of the account’s net liquidation value. This buffer percentage is crucial for deciding how much capital to trade based on the worst-case situations where the margin expands the most.

Step number 4) Calculate Planned Capital Allocation Per Tranche

To calculate the capital allocation per tranche, I use the Maximum Margin Requirement and the selected buffer percentage. For example, if the worst-case margin usage for one tranche is six thousand dollars, and I want to keep the worst-case margin expansion under 80 percent, I would allocate seven thousand five hundred dollars per tranche (six thousand dollars / 0 point 8 = seven thousand five hundred dollars). This ensures that I have enough capital to cover the potential margin expansion while still maintaining a safety net.

Step number 5) Stress Test the Positions Sizing Through Historical Scenarios

Finally, after determining the position sizing per tranche, I will then backtest the trading strategy again by examining its performance under different market scenarios, such as crash, grind-down, sideways, and up markets. This process helps me validate the effectiveness of my position sizing methodology and ensures that it can withstand various market conditions. By stress-testing the structure, I can make any necessary adjustments to my position sizing approach to better prepare for potential market upheavals.

Summary: My Process for Determining Optimal Position Sizing

Though it’s time-consuming, this process is essential for making informed decisions about position sizing. It’s important to note that every trader should tailor their own position sizing guidelines to align with their individual risk tolerance and account size, rather than simply copying someone else’s approach. Also, it is my opinion that, as an account grows larger, it’s appropriate to take less risk. In smaller accounts, traders might use more buying power and take on more risk, but as the account size increases, the percentage of buying power used could be decreased.
