Chapter 7: The Option Selling Market Types & the Greeks

Before we dive into the heart of the All-Market Profits Trading Framework, there’s one crucial aspect we need to first address: understanding the interplay between market types and the option Greeks.

No options trading book is complete without discussing the Greeks, and for good reason. Understanding the Greeks is crucial for traders to manage risk exposure as market conditions change.

In Chapter 4, we discussed how (on the surface) the key to all market profits is to have a trade structure that is:

  1. Positive Theta
  2. Flat Delta and Gamma
  3. Long Volatility (tamed Vega, long Vomma)

Now, first-order Greeks, like Delta, Theta, and Vega, are essential to understand, but solely focusing on these metrics alone is simply not enough to have a well-rounded trading strategy that can profit in all markets.

Typically, only paying attention to the first-order Greeks creates a deceivingly directional situation. This is why traders who only focus on the first-order Greeks can suffer drawdowns and stress and subsequently don’t outperform the S&P 500.

Instead, the secret is understanding the influences of higher- order Greeks such as Gamma, Charm, and Vomma. The trick is piecing together a trade structure and a trade plan that takes into account the four market types in conjunction with these higher-order Greeks.

In this chapter, I will detail as best I can why accounting for the real hidden market forces (the higher-order Greeks) is the key to all market profits and how understanding these market forces sets the stage for why the trade structure you’ll learn in Chapter 7 can achieve performance in all market conditions.

To accomplish this, I will:

  • Recap the Option Selling Market Types that a truly non-directional trading system must navigate.
  • Then, I will review the relevant Greeks that I believe are essential to understand if you wish to trade non-directionally.
  • From there, I will return to an analysis of the four market types that option sellers must contend with and show you - with an understanding of the higher-order Greeks - how you can set your trading up to overcome the challenges each market type presents.

Understanding the Options Selling Market Types

Before we dive into the Greeks, it is important to recap and understand the four main options selling market types as they relate to the overall market direction, trends, and volatility levels. When I say market types, I refer to the S&P 500 since I mainly trade options on the SPX and /ES.

For option premium sellers, the market environment can be broken down into four categories:

The Option Premium Selling Market Types
1. The Rising Bull - Market Rises With Relatively Low Volatility
For the entire year of 2017, there was a Rising Bull Market. The market (S P X or S&P 500 stock market) rose over 19% while the highest VIX reading was under 17.5.
2. The Sideways Market - Horizontal Range Bound Price Action With Relatively Low Volatility (and occasional tamed volatility spikes)
A Sideways Market occurred in the first six months of 2015 when the market stayed within a 4% range up or down and the VIX high was slightly above 23.
3. The Grind-Down Market - A Falling Bear Market With Tamed Volatility
A Grind-Down Market occurred from October 1, 2018, through December 31, 2018, when the market was down 14.5% with a maximum VIX spike of approximately 25 points. Another occurred from January 1, 2022, through October 12, 2022, when the market (S&P 500) drew down by -25.43%
4. The Crash Market - A fast bear market with a significant spike in volatility.
A Crash Market occurred in August 2015 when the market dropped 13.13% in four days and the VIX went from 15 to over 53, a 38-point spike. Another Crash Market occurred in early February 2018 when the market fell over 10.5% in seven days and the VIX went from 12.5 to over 50 in four days.

The key to long-term trading wealth is to have a trade structure and plan that preserves and grows your capital in all these market environments.

Each of these market types presents unique challenges for options traders, and understanding how the Greeks behave in each environment is crucial for success.

The Greeks

Now, let’s turn our focus to briefly defining the first-order Greeks - Delta, Theta, and Vega - which form the foundation of options trading as well as the higher-order Greeks, such as Gamma, Charm, and Vomma.

First-Order Greeks: The Foundation The first-order Greeks - Delta, Theta, and Vega - form the foundation of options trading. Here’s a brief overview of each and how it relates to the A.M.P. 1-2-2 Structure:

Theta: Represents the rate of time decay of an option. This is one of our edges. Premium sellers benefit from the passage of time (all else being equal) - this is “Theta decay.” To capitalize on this edge, we want to be Theta-positive, which helps weather all market types except for crashes. In a crash, another Greek (Vomma) takes over to make the trade structure profitable.

Delta: Represents the sensitivity of an option’s price to changes in the value of the underlying security. If Delta is not accounted for, you risk becoming directional and won’t be able to fully take advantage of Theta decay. Our goal is to be Delta-neutral to remove directional risk, meaning the market moving up or down has little effect on the P&L as far as Delta is concerned. Delta neutrality helps weather all market types except for crashes.

Vega: Represents an option’s sensitivity to volatility. If you are positive Theta, you are typically forced to be negative Vega - meaning that as volatility increases, the P&L will decrease - this is a trade-off that we have to manage. Without fully understanding Vega, you could make irrational trading decisions. We aim to mitigate the effects of Vega across all market types.

The A.M.P. 1-2-2 option structure leverages these first-order Greeks to create a more balanced and less directional trade. By carefully selecting the strikes and expiration dates, this structure can minimize the impact of Delta while benefiting from Theta decay and managing Vega risk.

Higher-Order Greeks: The Hidden Market Forces

While many options traders focus solely on the first-order Greeks, the real secret to success lies in understanding the influences of higher-order Greeks, such as Gamma, Charm, and Vomma.

Gamma: Represents the rate of change of Delta. Delta can be neutral, but price movements can still hurt you. Gamma- neutral trades maintain a stable Delta even when the underlying asset’s price changes, creating stability for every market type (except for crashes). Furthermore, if you’re Gamma- neutral, you can mitigate the effects of negative Vega because volatility expansions eventually revert to the mean.

Charm: Measures the rate of change of an option’s Delta concerning time. I think of Charm as the future “personality” of Delta and it needs to be taken into account to fully optimize a non-directional trade plan. We want negative Charm because our risk is to the downside. Negative Charm means that the Delta of the position will become more negative over time. This means if the market goes down, the position is making money based on negative Delta.

Vomma: Measures the sensitivity of an option’s Vega to changes in the implied volatility of the underlying asset. There’s only one asset guaranteed to rise in a market crash - volatility. By being positive Vomma, our trade structure can potentially make money during crashes.

The A.M.P. 1-2-2 option structure takes advantage of these higher-order Greeks to create a truly non-directional trade. By being Theta-positive, Delta and Gamma-neutral, having negative Charm, and being positive Vomma, this structure is designed to weather all market types and potentially profit during crashes.

Piecing it All Together: Understanding the Greeks in Different Market Types

Now that we’ve explored why success in options trading is contingent on not only an understanding of the first-order Greeks but also a deep appreciation for the higher-order Greeks, it’s time to switch gears and examine how these factors interact with various market conditions.

In the following sections, we’ll examine the Greeks through the lens of the four primary options selling market types with an emphasis on showcasing how the A.M.P. 1-2-2 trading structure can weather each.

Controlling the Greeks for Bull Market Performance

The way the structure does well in a bull market is by controlling the following Greeks:

Delta: If Delta is controlled, then the market’s movement upward effect on P&L can be better mitigated.

Gamma: Gamma is a second-order Greek. If Gamma is flat (or neutral) with the structure, then the market’s movement-up effect on P&L can be better mitigated. I think of Gamma as the curvature of the T plus 0 line in a risk graph.

Theta: Now, the position also carries positive Theta, which creates profit over time as the position has sold depreciating assets. This creates what I call positive headroom. Positive headroom simply means that the T+0 Line is lower than the next T+X Line coming up in time. With positive headroom, the position’s P&L is expected to increase in value over time all else being equal.

Vega: This positive Theta brings volatility risk to the trade, as Vega is negative. However, volatility does not typically increase in an underlying that climbs higher in a bull market.

Charm: If you’re an experienced options trader, you should be thinking that neutral Delta and neutral Gamma mean very little risk but also mean a lower chance for profit. This is where a second-order Greek, Charm, comes into play. The goal of a truly non-directional trade structure with more downside than upside risk is to have negative Charm, which means that, over time, a downside profit hump builds as the T+0 Line rises over time and eventually catches up to the underlying price in an up market.

Controlling the Greeks for Sideways Market Performance

Next, I’ll get into the sideways market. A sideways market is characterized by the underlying asset trading within a relatively tight range, with occasional volatility spikes. This type of market is really like a bull market on steroids for the “Proactively Hedged Long Put Broken Wing Butterfly.”

All the reasons a truly non-directional option strategy makes money in a bull market are the same for a sideways market, except profits can happen much faster as the negative Charm builds the profit hump and has less room to travel for the structure to make money. The depreciation of the options sold brings a truly non-directional strategy to profit and does so faster than in a bull market.

The “Proactively Hedged Long Put Broken Wing Butterfly” structure excels in this environment by leveraging the following Greek exposures:

Delta and Gamma:

The structure maintains a Delta-neutral position, ensuring that small price fluctuations within the range have minimal impact on the overall P&L. The flat Gamma exposure further reinforces the structure’s stability, as the Delta remains relatively unchanged, even as the underlying price moves within the range. This allows the trade to profit from time decay without being significantly affected by minor price movements.

Theta:

In a sideways market, the structure’s positive Theta exposure becomes even more advantageous. As time passes, the option positions in the structure decay in value, generating consistent profits for the trader. The longer the market remains range-bound, the more the trade benefits from Theta decay.

Vega:

Although the structure has a negative Vega exposure, the impact of volatility changes in a sideways market is typically less pronounced compared to other market conditions. The occasional volatility spikes may temporarily affect the trade’s P&L, but the overall impact is mitigated by the structure’s balanced design and the positive Theta exposure.

Charm:

The negative Charm exposure plays a crucial role in a sideways market. As time progresses, the negative Charm causes the downside profit hump the ability to reach the current market price compared to a bull market. This means that the trade can achieve profitability faster, even with minimal movement in the underlying asset’s price. The structure’s design takes advantage of this negative Charm, allowing it to generate profits more efficiently in a range-bound environment.

Controlling the Greeks for Grind-Down Market Performance

Grind-down markets pose significant challenges for options sellers. A grind-down market is characterized by a slow, persistent decline in the underlying asset’s price, often accompanied by moderate volatility levels. A notable example is the fourth quarter of 2018 where volatility, as measured by the VIX, rose to 36 and the S&P 500 declined by 20%. This scenario also unfolded throughout 2022.

In such markets, the underlying price often falls below the 3% to 7% buffer zone where Delta and Gamma are neutral.

Another typical feature of market downturns is an increase in volatility.

A truly non-directional strategy copes with grind-down markets by adapting to the aftermath of the initial drop. After the initial volatility shock stabilizes, the Vega in the position begins to recuperate the P&L as the negative Vega climbs back towards neutrality. Moreover, as the underlying price approaches the peak of the profit hump which builds to the downside, the strategy benefits.

It’s not only vital to keeping Delta under control but also to give the structure time to build a profit hump, which is crucial for protecting profits in a Grind-Down Market.

The “Proactively Hedged Long Put Broken Wing Butterfly” structure navigates this challenging environment by controlling the following Greeks:

Delta and Gamma:

As the market grinds down, the structure’s Delta exposure may shift slightly positively. However, the proactive hedging component and the careful strike selection help to maintain a relatively neutral Delta overall. The flat Gamma exposure ensures that the Delta remains stable, preventing the trade from becoming overly directional as the price declines.

Theta and Vega:

In a grind-down market, the positive Theta exposure continues to generate profits as time passes, offsetting some of the losses incurred from the increase in the underlying’s volatility. However, the negative Vega exposure becomes a more significant concern as volatility tends to increase during market declines. To manage this risk, the structure’s downside profit hump helps to mitigate the impact of rising volatility on the trade’s P&L.

Charm and Vomma:

The negative Charm exposure accelerates the buildup of the downside profit hump, allowing the trade to achieve profitability more quickly as the market grinds down. This is particularly beneficial in a grind-down market, as it helps to offset the losses from the increase in the underlying volatility. Additionally, the careful management of Vomma, which measures the rate of change of Vega concerning volatility, helps to optimize the trade’s performance during periods of elevated volatility.

Controlling the Greeks for Crash Market Performance

The final market scenario to consider is the Crash Market, characterized by the underlying price dropping 3%-7% beyond the flat Gamma point. The speed at which a crash occurs is a critical distinction between a grind-down market and a crash.

Notable examples of single-day crashes include August 24, 2015, and February 5, 2018. March 2020 presented a different situation with multiple consecutive crashes.

Truly non-directional option structures manage crashes by initially drawing down with the market but then capitalizing on Vomma. As the underlying volatility rises significantly, Vomma influences P&L above all other Greeks. I call this a “Vega Flip.”

Profit generation in crash scenarios hinges on Vomma, which denotes the exponential increase in Vega. This second-order Greek plays a critical role in the strategy’s effectiveness during market crashes.

The key is to have proactive hedges in place against market crashes.

Delta, Gamma, and Theta: During a market crash, the structure’s Delta exposure may temporarily shift to a more positive value as the rapid price decline pushes the trade towards a more directional exposure. However, the proactive hedging component and the strategic strike placement help to mitigate the impact of this sudden shift in Delta. The Gamma exposure, while relatively flat, may experience temporary fluctuations due to the extreme price movement. The positive Theta exposure, although still present, becomes less effective in generating profits during a sharp market decline.

Vega and Vomma:

This structure has negative Vega exposure and therefore, if the market draws down 3-7%, this structure will also draw down. However, when a market crash occurs, these hedges will begin to activate and this negative Vega will begin to flip. When volatility continues expanding, Vomma (the exponential part of Vega) will begin to kick in and a Vega Flip will occur. Then, because this structure is long volatility through the proactive hedges, the P&L of the structure will get into the positive. Therefore, in Black Swan events, this trade structure can do quite well because of these hedges.

Charm:

In a crash market, the mature negative Charm profit hump provides a potential buffer against the initial rapid price decline and volatility increase of the underlying. The effectiveness of Charm in generating profits during a crash market may be limited due to the extreme nature of the volatility expansion.

Conclusion

Many traders overlook the significance of higher-order Greeks, which are essential for managing the more familiar first-order Greeks, like Vega, Theta, and Delta.

The “Proactively Hedged Long Put Broken Wing Butterfly” (A.M.P. 1-2-2) option structure’s ability to control the Greeks across different market conditions is the key to its success in generating consistent profits and mitigating losses across all market conditions.

By maintaining a neutral Delta and flat Gamma profile, benefiting from positive Theta exposure, managing negative Vega risk, and leveraging negative Charm and Vomma exposures, the 1-2-2 structure can adapt to the unique challenges presented by each market type.
