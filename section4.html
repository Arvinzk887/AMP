As a non-professional trader trying to make your fortune from your own home, choosing which information sources you learn from is imperative for your education and your success. The problem?

Most Sources Are Either Biased, Not Transparent, or (worse) Unprofitable

TRADER PITFALL #2: BELIEVING STOCK MARKET SUCCESS COMES FROM PREDICTING

How many investors or traders do you know who can accurately pick the correct direction of a stock every time? I don’t know any.

How about most of the time? I know very few.

No doubt, accurately predicting the future direction of a stock price movement will make an investor money.

The problem is that this “attempt at prediction” is an investor’s most challenging task and is akin to predicting the future.

Certainly, there is a very small minority of investors who are highly skilled in fundamental analysis and/or technical analysis insofar as they can accurately predict future price movements, time their entries, properly manage their money around these directional bets, and consistently outperform the market.

I tip my hat to these individuals.

But, for the majority of investors? Attempting to predict direction is a losing proposition that can be summed up as “coin flipping exercises,” where guessing the market if a stock will go up or down is more akin to guessing if a coin toss will be heads or tails.

An analysis of the 5,292 trading days between 1996 and 2016 shows that the market was up 53.3% of the days and down 46.3% of those days.

If you need further proof, I highly recommend the book, A Random Walk Down Wall Street, by Burton Malkiel. In his book, Mr. Malkiel demonstrates that consistently predicting the future direction of stocks or assets has proven difficult.

This evidence further substantiates that even the world’s top stock pickers, hedge funds, and money managers have difficulty beating the market’s returns

So, what is the average person supposed to do if they wish to outperform the market?

Stop believing the lie that stock market success comes from predicting whether a stock will rise or fall.

The Achilles’ heel of most trading strategies is that they are directionally dependent. With these strategies, the profits and success lie in the trader or investor’s ability (or inability) to accurately predict the future.

A better approach is to turn your focus toward trading options instead of stocks. Options (in general) can be a more conservative and flexible investing vehicle where it is possible to construct strategies without the need to “time trades” or “predict” future price movements

How This Applies to the A.M.P. Framework

The A.M.P. trading strategies are all non-directional. In options, the term can be described as Delta-neutral - though a truly non-directional strategy is more than just Delta neutral as you’ll soon find out.

With a Delta-neutral strategy, the trader sets up a position where the Delta, which measures how much an option’s price moves in relation to the underlying asset’s price movement, is neutralized. Therefore, the strategy can profit despite the unpredictable nature of the stock market. You can mitigate the impact of price movements in the underlying asset while exploiting a statistical edge that options selling provides.

The best way for a majority of traders to make money is to sell option premiums because of the edge it provides. I call this the “Premiums Seller’s Edge.” If you are reading this, I am assuming you know that there is a statistical edge in selling options. If not, we will detail it further below.

The Premium Seller's Hedge.

PREMIUM SELLING is a term that means you sell more option premium than you buy.

In basic terms, selling option premiums is a lot like selling insurance. Rationally speaking, no one buys car insurance or health insurance to get rich. Yet when investors buy options they are buying insurance expecting to make money.

In reality, the ones making money are the insurance companies, and by selling premiums, you’re turning yourself into an insurance company.

Selling Options (selling premiums) Has Three Distinct Advantages:

#1 Options Are Overpriced:

One of the main pricing components of an option is Implied volatility (IV). Studies from the brokerage, tastytrade, have shown that implied volatility overstates realized volatility 75% of the time. This means that the price of the options is HIGHER than what they should be 75% of the time. Therefore, options are “typically” overpriced. This imbalance in options pricing presents opportunities for profit extraction when you sell the options, offering a more reliable path to consistent gains.

#2 Time Is on Your Side:

All options have one thing in common: they always expire. In other words, they are depreciating assets. Their value erodes as time passes. This is known as Theta decay. Positive Theta (or benefiting from the passage of time) can work IN YOUR FAVOR by utilizing the best premium selling options strategies.

#3 The Probabilities Are in Your Favor:

Selling options often have a higher probability of profit compared to buying options. Option sellers are taking on the role of an insurance provider for other market participants. This means that the “win rates” are higher.

TRADER PITFALL #5: NOT BEING TRULY DIVERSIFIED

The only free lunch in trading and investing is diversification - if you can pull it off.

This should be nothing new but in a broad sense, diversification (aka your asset allocation model) is not just how you manage risk, but it’s also the driving force behind your investment returns. When you properly manage risk, you limit your drawdowns, and you get better returns.

But, the devil is in the details, and many common diversification models are flawed in my opinion.

Common Diversification Attempts For Traditional Investors

For many investors and money managers, the common diversification approach is based on Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT). MPT posits that an investor can maximize investment returns while minimizing risk by considering the correlation between different assets.

Essentially, the goal is to create a “diversified” portfolio of different uncorrelated asset classes (such as stocks, bonds, etc.). When one draws down, the others make money. Therefore, you’re aiming to create better risk-adjusted returns compared to holding just one asset or just a couple of assets. It’s important to note, however, that systematic risk, which affects all investments (like risk arising from crashes or recession), cannot be eliminated through diversification, according to common approaches that utilize MPT.

Common Diversification Attempts For Option Investors

Now, for options traders, the concept of diversification takes on a different twist. When selling options, there are many “common” ways to diversify.

Diversify Across Different Underlyings: Go long or short on different uncorrelated underlyings across different sectors. Place trades on different stocks, ETFs, commodities, etc.

Diversify Across Different Strategies: Use different options strategies such as naked puts, credit spreads, debit spreads, iron condors, traditional butterflies, etc.

Diversify Across Different Time Periods: Some options traders will buy/sell long and short-dated options with the goal of diversifying their strategies across different events and time cycles.

Ultimately, many options traders “attempt” to diversify with much complexity through many positions - resulting in a ton of moving pieces that must be able to be properly managed and unwinded if unforeseen circumstances arise.

The Kryptonite

Regardless of the diversification, there is a kryptonite to “common” diversification approaches. It’s what happened in August of 2015. It’s what happened in February of 2018. It’s what happened during COVID. What is it?

Modern Crashes. With Modern Crashes, all assets become correlated.

This is called “crash correlation.”

Essentially, all assets are massively down while no asset class appears safe. Why is this the case? The answer is leverage and algorithms. There’s so much leverage in the system that a small downturn can force deleveraging, and everyone wants to be first to get out, so the algorithms trigger panic selling. This panic selling causes a crash and a significant volatility spike. This results in margin calls and forces one set of assets to be sold in order to cover another set of assets. This means that no “normal asset” is safe.

WHAT DOES THIS MEAN FOR THE AVERAGE PORTFOLIO?

The way 99% of asset managers apply the “Modern Portfolio Theory” breaks down due to this “systemic risk” because it relies on at least one uncorrelated asset to cover the losses, yet all normal assets in a crash are falling. This results in excess losses, even with portfolios that are supposedly uncorrelated. In other words, the supposed “diversification” turns into “diworsification.”

To be clear: it’s not that the theory itself doesn’t hold its weight. It’s the way that almost every money manager applies the theory. If I had most of my net worth invested in a typical “Modern Portfolio Theory” based approach, I would have trouble sleeping at night. That investment portfolio is too risky.

What This Means For Options Sellers:

“Crash Correlation” not only affects traditional diversification methods but also those who sell options. When your options portfolio consists of multiple correlated positions, it effectively becomes one large directional (short Vega) position, leading to high drawdowns, losses, and potential margin calls. That is not even including if the option seller has dozens of positions that they have to manage, adjust, or unwind in a fast-moving market.

What can be done?

The “secret” is to be long an asset that increases in value during a crash. There’s only one way to be LONG a crash. See, what everyone thinks they’re invested in is a diversified basket of assets. But, despite what many pundits will have you believe, there are only TWO asset classes. What they are actually invested in is either short or long volatility.

Long volatility is nothing new. Anyone can purchase protective hedges (such as buying deep out-of-the-money puts). The problem with going long volatility is the cost.

Every long volatility hedge has a cost to maintain it. In other words, the long volatility that will save your account when the crash comes has a cost to overcome during the noncrash periods. It is a tough balance to maintain, and most cannot do it.

However, as you will learn in the next few chapters when the proper options trade structure is used and managed properly, it is possible to create a more pure, predictable way to diversify a portfolio that can effectively mitigate risk while maximizing returns.

Let's recap the key points of this section and examine how All-Market Profits (A.M.P) strategies address these concerns:

✓ We Need to Diversify:

We cannot rely on traditional stock diversification strategies to weather all markets, so we turn to options. A.M.P. strategies harness “true diversification” by employing strategically constructed options structure that can be managed to perform well across various market conditions.

✓ We Need to Hedge against Tail Risk:

The primary purpose of a hedge is to protect capital in a down market, particularly a market that is crashing. The A.M.P. approach incorporates a proactive hedging component to mitigate the impact of tail risk events on the overall portfolio.

✓ It Needs to Be a Proactive Hedge:

During a crash, it’s too late to hedge. You may think you want to go short some stocks or short the market, but how do you know where the bottom is? As you’ll uncover, the A.M.P. strategy emphasizes the importance of having a proactive hedge in place before market turbulence strikes.

✓ It Needs to Be ‘Inexpensive’:

The problem with long volatility is the cost - or the drag on your portfolio. It can be difficult to justify 76 that cost when crashes may only occur once every three to five years. The A.M.P. approach seeks to optimize the cost-effectiveness of the hedging component by strategically integrating it into the overall options structure.

TRADER PITFALL #6: OVER RELYING ON DISCRETION, EMOTIONS, & SUBJECTIVITY

One of the biggest account killers for most traders is improperly managing the two biggest ‘emotions’ in trading: fear and greed.

Fear drives decisions that appear to avoid risk and generate little return, while greed drives decisions that appear to be too risky.

As a result, common wisdom suggests you need to master your “trading psychology” to be successful and profitable. There are hundreds of books, college classes, and even performance coaches dedicated to this topic.

I believe trading psychology is hogwash…when you have a proper plan.

The truth is, when you have a proper trading plan in place, you can effectively remove emotions from your trading decisions.

A well-defined trading plan should tell you what to trade, how and when to enter, how to manage your positions, and how and when to exit. A better trading plan does all that while also accounting for all market conditions. An ideal plan is backtested, simple, 100% mechanical, has optimal position sizing, creates time (not just money), and accounts for worst-case scenarios.

Unfortunately, most “trading plans” taught online are subjective. They might seem objective, but the educator typically leaves just enough “gray area” for discretion and nuance. This lack of clear, objective rules can lead to inconsistent results, as emotions will inevitably take over. Subjective trading, where rules are bent based on emotions and impulses, can be a recipe for disaster.

To ensure consistent profits, it’s crucial to create a clear and objective trading plan. Without a set of black-and-white rules, maintaining consistency becomes a challenge. Remember, success can only be consistent when the input – your trading decisions – is consistent.

The Benefits of a Mechanical, Quantitative Investing Approach

A key reason for the success of the “All-Market Profits” strategies method’s success lies in its emphasis on quantitative trading. By relying on data-driven decision-making and objective rules, traders can remove the subjectivity that often leads to emotional trading. Quantitative investing allows for a more mechanical, systematic approach to trading that is grounded in statistical analysis and historical data.

One of the key benefits of a quantitative approach is the ability to backtest strategies. Backtesting involves evaluating a trading strategy’s performance using historical data, which can help validate its effectiveness and identify potential weaknesses. By relying on backtested strategies, traders can have greater confidence in their trading plan and reduce the need for subjective judgment calls.

Conclusion

In summary, most trading approaches can be divided into two categories: Discretionary Trading and Systematic Trading. Discretionary trading is more subjective and relies on human judgment, while systematic trading is objective, relying on predefined rules.

In my opinion, relying on discretion, emotions, and subjectivity is counterproductive for long-term trading success - especially when first starting out. The All-Market Profits method provides a powerful framework for achieving this goal, offering a mechanical, rule-based approach that eliminates the need for subjective decision-making. By adopting a quantitative investing approach, backtesting strategies, and adhering to a well-defined trading plan, traders can navigate the challenges of emotional trading and achieve more consistent, profitable results.

As you progress through this book, you’ll learn more about the specific components of the “All-Market Profits” Trading Framework and how they work together to create a robust, emotion-free trading system. By implementing these principles and committing to a mechanical, rule-based approach, you can take control of your trading performance and achieve the long-term success you desire.

TRADER PITFALL #7: Complexity Fails, Simple Scales

The last pitfall that many traders make is overcomplicating their strategy.

Options trading offers incredible flexibility, which can be both a blessing and a curse. With hundreds of strategies and thousands of variations, such as straddles, strangles, condors, iron condors, butterflies, and iron butterflies, it’s easy to get overwhelmed by the sheer number of choices.

Take one look under the hood at option-selling portfolios from traders at tastytrade or YouTube. You might see dozens of positions at one time, each with its own unique risk profile, expiration dates, management criteria, and capital requirements.

Seeing other people trade so many strategies simultaneously can tempt both novice and experienced traders to create a complex web of overcomplicated, over-diversified, and difficult- to-manage strategies in their own portfolios.

The allure of changing up strategies or trying new approaches based on someone else’s successful P&L can be strong. However, this temptation can lead to overwhelm and can be counterproductive to the primary goal of trading: creating time, not just money. Most traders don’t have the luxury of sitting in front of their computers all day actively managing their accounts. Doing so would turn trading into another fulltime job, which defeats the purpose of passively growing wealth.

Moreover, juggling an overcomplicated portfolio of different positions and strategies not only requires more screen time but also makes you more prone to making suboptimal management decisions, especially during market downturns. In options trading, making money relies not only on having a great strategy but also on effectively managing that strategy when the market becomes volatile.

If you don’t fully understand how to manage all your strategies, it can be particularly problematic in a highly volatile crash environment. During these times, effectively managing and unwinding all of your positions can be extremely difficult and stressful, potentially leading to massive losses. I learned this expensive lesson the hard way during the flash crash of 2018 when I had to leg in and out of positions, wiping out months of gains.

My advice is simple: master simplicity before earning the right to complexity.

Avoid chasing “shiny objects” until you have mastered one or two core strategies. Go a mile deep with your strategy rather than a mile wide across dozens of “half-baked” strategies. Remember, the real money in options trading comes from how you manage your strategies. Don’t become a jack of all trades and a master of none.

Focus on developing a deep understanding of a simple, yet effective strategy that aligns with your goals and risk tolerance. By doing so, you’ll be better equipped to navigate market volatility and make sound management decisions when the time comes. As you gain experience and confidence, you can gradually expand your repertoire, but always prioritize mastery over complexity.
