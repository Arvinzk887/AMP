Quick Check-In

As we wrap up this section of the chapter, I want to ask you thought-provoking questions:

  1. To what extent do your trading strategies focus on prediction over other analysis methods?
  2. How successful have you been in the past with trades based on directional predictions?

Take a moment to honestly reflect on your answers.

If you can consistently and successfully predict the future direction of a stock and manage money based on those predictions, then you may not need the insights provided in this book.

However, if you find that your prediction-based trades have yielded subpar results or if you’re looking for a more reliable approach to navigate the markets, then the principles outlined in Part 2 of this book can help you explore a less prediction- dependent path to trading success.

TRADER PITFALL Number 3: CHOOSING A TRADING STRATEGY WITH A LOW PROBABILITY OF SUCCESS

By now, you hopefully know the antidote for cutting through B.S. trading advice is rather simple - get a verifiable profit and loss statement and/or backtested performance. In other words, no P&L, no discussion.

This should eliminate 95 percent of the fake gurus online. Even then, our job is not done.

As with any type of investment, only some of the trading strategies will be appropriate for your objective. A particular strategy is “successful” only if it helps you meet your investment goals.

There are myriad ways to make money in the market, ranging from buy-and-hold, day trading, and forex to stock options and beyond. Whether they’re directional approaches or not, there are and always will be people who will make particular strategies for them.

That said, even if someone is profitable does not mean that their methods will work for you. Many people misjudge the true likelihood of success in common trading methods. Below I’ve listed why I think many of the “popular” trading strategies are ill-advised (beyond the obvious answer that many of them are directional).

The Perception Deception in Mainstream Trading Strategies

The Data On Day Trading - Directional

The internet is rife with day trading experts peddling their “magic wand indicators” and courses promising to unlock the secret to windfall profits from only a few hours of daily work. Yet, studies show that an astonishingly few day traders can profit over time consistently, and those that do are extremely experienced and sophisticated -- not beginners.

In fact, it is virtually impossible for an individual to day trade for a living contrary to what brokerage specialists and course providers often claim. Another study showed less than 1 percent of the day trader population can predictably and reliably earn positive, abnormal returns net of fees.

Penny Stock Pump & Dump - Directional

In my opinion, penny stocks are generally nothing less than scams. The truth is these stocks cost pennies for a reason, nobody wants to buy them. Most pump-and-dump and “front running” schemes happen with penny stocks, and there’s a good chance that if you do buy penny stocks, you won’t be able to find a seller for them when you want to get out. They just don’t have enough volume. The odds of being able to trade penny stocks for a profit consistently are slim to none.

Crypto Craziness - Directional

Just like penny stocks, cryptocurrency is a highly speculative market fraught with insider trading, bogus training, and thugs. The combination of extreme market volatility, security vulnerabilities, and fraudulent activities, such as Ponzi schemes and “rug pull” scams, can lead to substantial losses for investors. The lack of robust regulation in this sector leaves investors vulnerable and without recourse in cases of theft or fraud - a case in point is the 2022 implosion of popular Crypto exchange FTX and the subsequent guilt of founder Sam Bankman-Fried.

Forex Forays - Directional

If you’re considering trading FOREX, think again. Forex is extremely unpredictable and unreliable because it’s based on governments and international relationships.

It doesn’t play by the same rules as the other financial instruments, making it too risky for us. A study by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission of forex traders found that 70 percent of traders lose money every quarter on average, and traders typically lose 100 percent of their money within 12 months. A good rule of thumb is if you wanna lose your money, go into FOREX.

Buying Options - Directional

Fueled partly by commission-free brokerages, Reddit users, and Meme Stockholders, buying options have gained popularity in the last few years. Small investors that buy options are almost always net losers. Buying options is a nuanced game of probabilities, and the odds are rarely on your side. Not only do they have to be right directionally but the magnitude of the move must exceed the expected move, which rarely happens. Perhaps most importantly, when you buy options, you fight time decay. The value of options decreases in value as time goes on (all else being equal).

Rationally speaking, buying options is like gambling. The casino is the one that makes the money, not the gambler

In conclusion, everyone loves a good story about how someone “turned ten thousand dollars into millions,” but the reality is that a very (very) small percentage of people are successful with the aforementioned strategies. Be cautious when an educator touts about the very select few students who become successful with their strategy and consider that many of these companies sell thousands of courses and only about 10 or so students become successful. The odds are simply stacked against you when following the aforementioned strategies.

Instead, you should ask yourself if the probabilities are stacked in your favor that you will succeed with your trading or investing strategy.

Probabilities are everything when it comes to trading success, and that is why I believe the only ways to make money for a majority of retail investors are to:

  1. Buy and hold over a long period of time or…
  2. Sell option premiums.

High Probability Strategy Number 1: Buy & Hold

If your financial goal over the long run is to have a decent return with medium volatility — and you value the ability to track the markets on a day-to-day basis closely — then lowfee, passive market-cap indices can be a great option.

The problem is in the term “over the long run.” Buy-and-hold strategies tend only to work if you have a long time horizon for investing. For example, had you invested in the S&P 500 at the peak of the dot-com bubble in March of 2000, it would have taken you up until 2014 to recover. Had you invested in October 2007 (right before the Great Recession), you would have had to wait five-and-a-half years to break even on your investment.

For buy-and-hold strategies, the best defense against significant losses is simply having a long enough time horizon. This doesn’t cut it for me because I don’t want to wait 5, 10, or 15 plus years for a substantial return and it shouldn’t cut it for you either if your goal is to trade for a living, supplement your income, or bankroll your retirement.
