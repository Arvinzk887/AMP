TRADER PITFALL Number 5: NOT BEING TRULY DIVERSIFIED

The only free lunch in trading and investing is diversification - if you can pull it off.

This should be nothing new but in a broad sense, diversification (aka your asset allocation model) is not just how you manage risk, but it’s also the driving force behind your investment returns. When you properly manage risk, you limit your drawdowns, and you get better returns.

But, the devil is in the details, and many common diversification models are flawed in my opinion.

Common Diversification Attempts For Traditional Investors

For many investors and money managers, the common diversification approach is based on Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT). MPT posits that an investor can maximize investment returns while minimizing risk by considering the correlation between different assets.

Essentially, the goal is to create a “diversified” portfolio of different uncorrelated asset classes (such as stocks, bonds, etc.). When one draws down, the others make money. Therefore, you’re aiming to create better risk-adjusted returns compared to holding just one asset or just a couple of assets. It’s important to note, however, that systematic risk, which affects all investments (like risk arising from crashes or recession), cannot be eliminated through diversification, according to common approaches that utilize MPT.

Common Diversification Attempts For Option Investors

Now, for options traders, the concept of diversification takes on a different twist. When selling options, there are many “common” ways to diversify.

Diversify Across Different Underlyings: Go long or short on different uncorrelated underlyings across different sectors. Place trades on different stocks, ETFs, commodities, etc.

Diversify Across Different Strategies: Use different options strategies such as naked puts, credit spreads, debit spreads, iron condors, traditional butterflies, etc.

Diversify Across Different Time Periods: Some options traders will buy/sell long and short-dated options with the goal of diversifying their strategies across different events and time cycles.

Ultimately, many options traders “attempt” to diversify with much complexity through many positions - resulting in a ton of moving pieces that must be able to be properly managed and unwinded if unforeseen circumstances arise.

The Kryptonite

Regardless of the diversification, there is a kryptonite to “common” diversification approaches. It’s what happened in August of 2015. It’s what happened in February of 2018. It’s what happened during COVID. What is it?

Modern Crashes. With Modern Crashes, all assets become correlated.

Here is what happened from March 6 to March 19, 2020 COVID Crash Market:

GLD (GOLD) Negative 12.21 percent

TLT (BONDS) Negative 11.19 percent

SPY (INDEX) Negative 19.26 percent

AAPL (TECH) Negative 8.8 percent

BA (DEFENSE) Negative 62 percent

TGT (RETAIL) Negative 5.4 percent

LUV (TRAVEL) Negative 33 percent

OXY (OIL) Negative 60 percent

AMGN (BIO) Negative 6 percent

This is called “crash correlation.”

Essentially, all assets are massively down while no asset class appears safe. Why is this the case? The answer is leverage and algorithms. There’s so much leverage in the system that a small downturn can force deleveraging, and everyone wants to be first to get out, so the algorithms trigger panic selling. This panic selling causes a crash and a significant volatility spike. This results in margin calls and forces one set of assets to be sold in order to cover another set of assets. This means that no “normal asset” is safe.
