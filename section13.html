Chapter 7.5: Trade Management Rules

In the previous chapters, we discussed the essential components of the All-Market Profits Trading Framework, including the construction of the All-Market Profits 1-2-2 option structure, the advantages of using SPAN or Portfolio Margin, the importance of proper position sizing, and guidelines for executing trades. It’s time to incorporate these learnings and form a trade management plan.

A trade management plan is a comprehensive set of rules and guidelines that define how you approach the markets. It covers every aspect of your trading process, from the instruments you trade to the specific criteria for entering, managing, and exiting positions. A good trading plan is designed to optimize your trading structure’s effectiveness while minimizing risk and emotional interference.

There are several vital reasons for having a trading management plan:

  • Consistency: A well-defined trading plan ensures that you approach the markets consistently, regardless of your emotional state or recent performance. By following a set of objective rules, you can avoid impulsive decisions and maintain discipline in your trading.
  • Risk Management: A comprehensive trade plan incorporates risk management strategies, such as position sizing, stop-loss levels, and adjustment criteria. Adhering to these rules can protect your capital and limit your downside risk during adverse market conditions.
  • Accountability: Having a written trade plan holds you accountable to a specific set of rules and guidelines. It prevents you from deviating from your strategy or making excuses for poor performance, as you can always refer back to your plan to assess whether you followed it correctly.
  • Continuous Improvement: A trade plan is not a static document; it should evolve as you gain experience and refine your approach. By regularly reviewing your plan’s performance and making data-driven adjustments, you can continuously improve your trading results over time.

The Basic Questions a Trade Plan Should Answer

At the very least, here are the questions a trading plan should answer:

  • What underlying to trade: Determine which instruments you will focus on, such as stocks, options, futures, or a combination thereof.
  • Entry criteria: Define the specific conditions under which you will enter a trade, such as timing, particular price levels, technical indicators, or market events.
  • Position sizing: Establish clear rules for determining the size of your positions based on your account balance, risk tolerance, and the specific characteristics of each trade.
  • Adjustment criteria: Specify the circumstances that will trigger adjustments to your positions, such as changes in directional risk, price movements, volatility, or time decay.
  • Exit criteria: Determine the conditions under which you will exit a trade, including profit targets, stop-loss levels, and time-based exits or time stops.

An Example Trade Plan

Here's a simplified example of a basic trade plan:

  • Instrument: SPY options.
  • Strategy: Iron Condor
  • Position sizing: 2 percent of account balance per trade.
  • Entry criteria: 45 days to expiration, short strikes at one standard deviation, and long strikes 10 dollars further out of the money.
  • Adjustment criteria: Adjust when the short strike is touched or at 21 days to expiration.
  • Exit criteria: 50 percent of maximum profit or 200 percent of initial credit at risk.
  • Risk management: No more than 3 iron condors open at once, max loss per trade 1 percent of account balance.

While a common trade plan might provide a basic framework, many often lack the specificity and adaptability needed to navigate diverse market conditions. In contrast, a trade plan is designed to be comprehensive, mechanical, and adaptable, focusing on risk management and consistent performance across various market scenarios.

Here are examples of unique triggers I incorporate into my trade management plans:

  • Delta management: Adjusting the position when the Delta exceeds a certain threshold per tranche helps maintain a neutral Delta exposure and minimize directional risk. This trigger ensures that the trade remains non-directional, even as market conditions change.
  • Opportunistic adjustments: Adding new layers on market dips for potential profit expansion. This trigger enables the strategy to capitalize on market volatility and enhance overall profitability.
  • VIX-based adjustments: Modifying strategy parameters when the VIX exceeds a certain level helps adapt the plan to changing market volatility and protect against extreme moves. This trigger ensures that the trade remains well-positioned, even during periods of heightened uncertainty.

Next Steps:

It is important to note that there is not necessarily a onesize- fits-all trade management plan, as each trader has their own set of goals, risk tolerances, and experience levels. The example I gave above is just that - an example. In no way is this a recommended way to manage a proactively hedged broken wing butterfly (A.M.P. 1-2-2).

That said, to create your own comprehensive trade management plan, start by defining your goals and the specific trade structure you will use. Then, develop clear, objective rules for entry, adjustment, and exit criteria, incorporating risk management techniques such as position sizing and campaign- style trading.

The next step is to extensively backtest your plan across various market conditions to ensure its robustness and adaptability. By developing a comprehensive, mechanical trade management plan tailored to your specific goals and trade structure, you can optimize the effectiveness of your trading and achieve consistent, stress-free profitability in all market conditions.
