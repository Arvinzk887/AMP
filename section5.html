Chapter 4: The Key to Truly Non-Directional, All-Market Trading Profits (and Why Many Fall Short)

In the previous chapter, we explored seven costly pitfalls that hinder successful trading and investing. However, one of the most significant obstacles to long-term options trading success is not utilizing an options structure that consistently generates profits in ALL market conditions: bull, bear, sideways, and crashes.

To thrive in any market environment, I believe the first step is to adopt a versatile, comprehensive option structure that accomplishes several things:

  • It capitalizes on Theta decay.
  • It effectively eliminates directional risk
  • It is proactively hedged against volatility risk.

The issue lies in the fact that most “traditional” options structures, such as iron condors, spreads, and butterflies, often carry hidden directional risk, rendering them ineffective in harnessing the edge provided by selling options.

In this chapter, I will unveil components of one of my preferred options structures that I believe is the key to achieving truly non-directional trading profits. This structure is the cornerstone for the full “All-Market Proftis” framework that you’ll learn in Part 2 of this book.

With that said, before delving into the specifics of one of my preferred option structures, I believe it’s crucial to take a step back and clarify the “common” definition of non-directional trading as understood by most options traders. From there, I will explain why I believe many traditional “non-directional” structures are deceivingly directional.

Next, I will highlight the advantages a broken wing butterfly (BWB) options structure offers as a foundation for non-directional profits, while also addressing its limitations. Finally, I’ll present why adding a proactive hedge to the BWB is crucial for achieving true non-directionality.

What is the “common” definition of non-directional trading?

As we know, directional trading strategies are a common reason most people fail to profit consistently in the market.

Why? Directional trading involves making directional bets on the future price movements of a stock and predicting this direction is difficult at best and impossible at worst.

So we turn to selling options because they offer the potential to make money without the need to accurately predict the future direction of the stock. Options are depreciating assets, and their value tends to decay over time (Theta decay).

One key to successful options trading is capturing this depreciation while avoiding directional risk. In other words, we want to harness the Premium Seller’s Edge without movements in the underlying overpowering that edge. This is commonly referred to as removing directional risk or Delta risk.

Directional (Delta) risk is significant because, for sellers of options, the value of the option they sold (and potentially need to buy back or settle) can increase significantly if the underlying asset moves in the wrong direction.

Avoiding directional risk is where the term “non-directional” comes from. In a broad sense, non-directional trading strategies “attempt” to generate consistent returns without needing to predict the market’s direction accurately (though most are still directional to some degree).

Does Non-Directional Mean Delta-Neutral?

If you’re a seasoned options enthusiast, you might have heard of non-directional trading referred to as Delta-neutral trading.

Common Delta-neutral strategies like strangles, iron condors, and butterflies involve balancing the Delta of each leg to equal zero, making them appear non-directional at first glance.

In theory, these “Delta-neutral” strategies might seem non-directional because the underlying asset can move slightly up, down, or remain sideways, and the trade can still hit its profit target.

However, in practice, I argue that these strategies are deceivingly directional because they still rely on prediction to some degree.

Take the Iron Condor for example:

In this case, the trader is betting that the stock will remain sideways, go slightly up, or go slightly down. The strategy’s success depends on the underlying asset’s price staying within a specific range. Significant moves outside that range will lead to losses, undermining the non-directional intent.

Moreover, traditional Delta-neutral structures are only Delta- neutral for a single moment in time. If the underlying moves slightly against you, the structure is no longer Delta- neutral, revealing that many of these structures are often just predictional bets on the underlying remaining in a tight range, making them deceivingly directional.

True non-directionality requires more than just balancing Deltas. It means having a structure that profits in all four key options selling market regimes, not just one or two. It also means accounting for higher-order Greeks, like Gamma and Vomma (covered in Chapter 6), not just Delta.

So, what is a robust yet flexible starting structure that can form the foundation for truly non-directional trading? I believe a good starting point is a Long Put Broken Wing Butterfly (BWB).

The Broken Wing Butterfly: Why I Prefer It as the Foundational Structure for All-Market Profits?

A Long Put Broken Wing Butterfly (BWB) is an effective starting structure for truly non-directional trading, consisting of all out-of-the-money (OTM) puts.

This structure involves:

  • Long one OTM put at a higher strike - less OTM than the middle.
  • Short two OTM puts at the middle strike.
  • Long one OTM put at a lower strike - more OTM than the middle.

This asymmetric arrangement of strikes forms the broken wing of the butterfly spread.

There are several advantages to this structure as a starting point.

Advantage Number 1: Positive Theta & Headroom

The structure is positive Theta meaning that it benefits from the passage of time (time decay). Positive Theta can show up if you look at a long put broken wing butterfly structure on a risk graph through what I call “positive headroom.”

Positive headroom is easily understood by examining a risk graph. It shows how the profit and loss (P&L) is expected to trend upwards, moving into positive territory, assuming all other factors remain constant. Simply put, if the market conditions stay the same, the P&L will likely increase.

In this scenario, the current position (marked by the blue dot) has positive headroom. Given that the T+0 Line is lower than the next T+X Lines, the position is expected to increase in value over time (all else being equal). This is positive headroom.

Advantage Number 2: Potentially Zero Upside Risk

A Long put Broken Wing Butterfly (BWB) can be set up to define the upside risk. In a scenario where the stock price increases significantly, the maximum risk is defined. If the position is set up with a net credit, the trader may keep the credit as profit if all options expire worthless. If it’s set up with a net debit, this debit represents the maximum loss. The upside risk can also be mitigated further through effective management.

Advantge Number 3: Delta Neutrality & Flat Gamma Lines

At trade entry, the BWB can be Delta-neutral with flat Gamma. This means it is not only non-directional but has a stable Delta that is less sensitive to price movement than other spreads.

We will discuss the importance of flat Gamma in future chapters. That said, when looking at a long-put BWB on a risk graph, the slope of the T+0 Line is flat. Having flat T+0 Lines is indicative of both Delta neutrality and Gamma neutrality.

Advantage Number 4: Long Runway plus Margin of Safety

Runway refers to the extent of price fluctuation an underlying asset can undergo before substantially affecting the position’s profit and loss (P&L). A position with a longer runway can tolerate greater price changes before there’s a notable impact on the P&L, while a shorter runway indicates less tolerance to price shifts. A flatter Gamma curve indicates a longer runway.

In this example of a butterfly position, the current position (indicated by the red dot) is further away from the tent (upper long strike). This presents a longer runway - the margin of safety before direction overcomes the Premium Seller’s Edge.

Advantage Number 5: Exploitation of Higher-Order Greeks

As you’ll discover in Part 2, second-order Greeks, like Gamma, Charm, Vomma, and Vanna, are the key to being truly non-directional. Only managing first-order Greeks, like Delta, Theta, and Vega, is not enough to mine the Premium Seller’s Edge. A structure like the BWB can help you control and exploit the second-order Greeks to your advantage.

The Traditional Broken Wing Butterfly IS INCOMPLETE

While the long put broken wing butterfly offers distinct advantages that can set the foundation for truly non-directional success, I believe it is incomplete for one critical reason.

Because a traditional put broken wing butterfly is short volatility, crash environments and black swan events can significantly impact the profitability of this structure due to the significant tail risk.

That is why I believe it’s crucial to modify this structure to mitigate volatility and Vega risk by being LONG volatility through a built-in proactive hedge.

Black Swan & Crash Environments: The Need for a Proactive “Built-In” Hedge

While the long put broken wing butterfly offers distinct advantages that can set the foundation for truly non-directional success, it’s crucial to recognize its limitations. As a standalone strategy, a traditional put broken wing butterfly is inherently short volatility, leaving it vulnerable to significant drawdowns during crash environments and black swan events.

To effectively navigate these turbulent market conditions, it’s essential to incorporate a proactive hedge that mitigates volatility risk by maintaining long volatility exposure. The primary purpose of this hedge is to protect capital during market downturns, particularly when “crash correlation” comes into play, and asset prices across the board are plummeting.

The key lies in being proactively long volatility before a crash occurs. Attempting to hedge reactively once a market decline is underway is often too late, as the cost of protective puts skyrockets and liquidity evaporates. Moreover, trying to time the bottom or predict the extent of the selloff with reactive hedges can lead to whipsawing, further stress, and additional losses.

To preserve capital and sleep well at night, the optimal approach is to be proactively hedged and already own the one asset that everyone wants in a crash – long volatility exposure. However, it’s equally important to ensure that the options portfolio is still profitable during non-crash market scenarios.

Therein lies the challenge: the cost of maintaining the long volatility hedge that will save your account during a crash needs to be offset by profits generated during normal market conditions.

Striking this delicate balance requires a meticulously designed options trade structure that capitalizes on Theta decay and mitigates volatility risk simultaneously. The structure must be able to weather the storm during crashes while still generating profits in calmer markets.

Now, it’s time to bring it all together and reveal the cornerstone structure that I believe is the key to achieving consistent profits in any market condition: the proactively hedged broken wing butterfly - or what I like to call the All-Market Profits 1-2-2 option structure (the A.M.P. 1-2-2).

The Cornerstone "All-Market Proftits" Trading Structure

The basic building blocks of the All-Market Profits 1-2-2 are the same as those of the long put broken wing butterfly. However, instead of only one long put at the lower strike, we now have TWO long puts at the lower strike. This means that there are now 3 long puts and 2 short puts.

I will reveal the exact criteria (including Delta, DTEs, etc.) for the structure in Chapter 7 of this book. However, on a surface level, when this structure is constructed correctly, it has all of the advantages of the Long Put Broken Wing Butterfly previously covered, along with several other crucial components for all market profits - primarily the built-in hedge.

Additional Advantage Number 1: Long Volatility - More Long Puts Than Short Puts

One of the keys for a truly non-directional option structure is to have more long puts than short puts. How this is expressed in the All-Market Profits 1-2-2 structure is to have two long puts on the lower leg. Again, I’ll reveal the exact details in Chapter 7.

With more long puts than short puts, the structure maintains a long volatility exposure, which is essential for profiting during crash environments when volatility spikes. The exact dynamics of this phenomenon have to do with the higher-order Greek, Vomma (which will be explained thoroughly in Chapter 6).

Additional Advantage Number 2: The Hedge is "Built-in"

The protective hedge is seamlessly integrated into the structure, eliminating the need to manage separate hedging positions. This streamlines trade management and reduces the complexity of the overall portfolio.

Additional Advantage Number 3: Designed to Hang Out in Profit Hump

The proactively hedged broken wing butterfly (A.M.P. 1-2- 2) is designed to maximize time spent in the “Profit Hump” area of the risk graph. “Profit Humps” are points on a risk graph where the T plus X Lines experience changes in slope, resulting in a price area where the position is in its maximum potential profitability. By carefully selecting strike prices and expiration dates, it is possible to optimize the structure to capitalize on this key region.

By combining the benefits of positive Theta, flat Delta and Gamma, and long volatility exposure, the All-Market Profits 1-2-2 structure emerges as a cornerstone structure for traders seeking consistent profits in all market conditions.

It’s important that while this structure serves as a robust starting point for building a non-directional options trading strategy, it is just that - a starting point. The structure must be adapted to account for varying market conditions, volatility levels, and the specific goals of the individual trader.
