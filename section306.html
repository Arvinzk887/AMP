Chapter 3: The 7 Costly Pitfalls Preventing All-Market Trading Success

Trading is not easy. It’s made infinitely more complex by a variety of factors.

After over three decades of trading and spending thousands on trading courses and personally working with hundreds of traders, I’ve learned things you can only learn by trying, failing, and trying again.

I want to share with you the seven pitfalls both aspiring traders and experienced traders make when aiming for longterm success.

I want to share these for two reasons:

  1. So that you can learn from these mistakes and avoid making them yourself.
  2. To show you why I’m passionate about using truly non-directional “All-Market Profits” trading strategies to protect and grow portfolios.

Some of these pitfalls may hit close to home. Others may not seem relevant to you - yet. But, believe me, if you want lasting success as an options trader, you need to be (at least) cognizant of them all.

While the chapter is extensive, I believe the knowledge acquired will be invaluable as you progress through this book and work towards achieving consistent profitability in various market conditions. Take the time to fully absorb the information presented, as it will serve you well when applying the “All-Market Profits” framework in Part 2 of this book.

Let’s begin!

TRADER PITFALL number 1: CHOOSING THE WRONG INFORMATION SOURCE

As a non-professional trader trying to make your fortune from your own home, choosing which information sources you learn from is imperative for your education and your success. The problem?

Most Sources Are Either Biased, Not Transparent, or (worse) Unprofitable

Source number 1: The Stock-Picking Media Machine

On the one hand, you have the “talking heads” on cable news networks who offer an endless stream of (often conflicting) stock-picking advice. These media personalities come with an “impressive” resume from Wall Street top firms. They use a mix of complex jargon and selective analysis to lead the novice “return chasing” investor to believe their word for gold.

In reality, their incentive is more aligned with padding their ego and their companies’ wallets rather than dishing out credible investing advice.

Take, for example, the most notable television stock picker of all, CNBC’s “Mad Money” host, Jim Cramer. Research studies have shown that over 17 plus years reviewed, Jim Cramer has underperformed the S&P 500 total return index both on an overall returns basis and in the Sharpe ratio.1

Therefore, it’s advisable to view these programs more as a source of entertainment than as a reliable foundation for investment education.2

Source number 2: The Wild West of Online Trading Educators or Gurus

The emergence of social media has ushered in a “Wild West” of self-proclaimed trading and investment educators who promise riches but aren’t actually profitable themselves. These educators are more focused on selling the dream of wealth than delivering actual, profitable trading strategies.

In fact, I believe (at least) 95 percent of the trading educators on the internet are overhyped “snake oil” salesmen, grifters, and con artists meant to hustle you out of your time and hard-earned money. They, unfortunately, use slick, deceptive advertising to prey on desperate people who want to “get rich quick” without effort.

I would know because I went on a crusade and publicly reviewed 30 plus online trading courses on my YouTube channel. On the channel, I investigate many of the well-known trading gurus and their strategies in an effort to bring the best trading strategies to light and to help the average consumer avoid wasting their money and time. A few of these gurus have threatened me with cease and desist letters and I’ve even been sued.

Now, why would I spend my time researching and litigating if I have my own strategies that work?

  • Unselfish Reason: My mission is to add value and ensure that you’re successful regardless if you decide to learn with me or not. If I can help save you time, money, and effort by avoiding scams, I’ve done my job.
  • Selfish Reason: I am fanatical about perfecting my trading so if a person is touting a “better” strategy, I’ll investigate to see if they do something I am not doing so I can implement it myself. 99 percent of the time, I come up empty-handed.
