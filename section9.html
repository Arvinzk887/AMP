Chapter 7 point 1: The Exact Construction of the A.M.P. 1-2-2 Option Structure

In the previous chapters, we’ve laid the groundwork as to why I believe a “Proactively Hedged Put Broken Wing Butterfly” (The A.M.P. 1-2-2) is a great starting point when aiming for all market profits due to positive Theta, flat Delta and Gamma, and long volatility exposure.

Now, it’s time to dive into how I would construct this trade structure, including the underlyings, widths, and expirations.

First Here Is a Quick Recap & Overview of the Structure:

Construction Principles:

Crafting the ideal options structure for all market conditions is no easy feat. It requires countless hours of backtesting, analyzing, and refining to find the perfect balance between risk and reward.

Throughout my trading career, I’ve conducted over 10,000 backtests on various options strategies, meticulously studying their performance across different market environments. The exact details of the A.M.P. 1-2-2 options structure I’m about to share with you are the result of this rigorous process - a culmination of years of trial and error, optimization, and fine-tuning.

By following the construction principles outlined next, you’ll be able to shortcut your way to a proven, reliable options structure that has the potential to weather any storm the market throws your way.

Underlying:

When it comes to selecting the underlying asset for your options structure, liquidity and cost efficiency should be top priorities. That’s why I prefer to trade options on broad market indexes such as the SPX or the forward slash ES E-mini S&P 500 futures. These underlyings offer several key advantages.

Advantage Number 1: Liquidity & Commissions

Liquidity and slippage should be paramount concerns when trading. If you have a position in a stock or equity option with little volume, you could be trapped into paying an exorbitant amount of slippage.

I focus my options trading on the most liquid, cost-effective equity underlying SPX. You can trade options on the SPY, which is very liquid but only 1/10 the size of SPX, or the E mini futures symbol /ES, which is half the size of SPX.

SPX has the advantage of lower commissions because I can pay for commissions to trade 1 SPX contract and get the leverage of trading 10 SPY contracts. If I pay to trade 10 SPY contracts, I would pay 10 times the commissions.

While that seems obvious, it’s very important to not overlook the role commissions have when factoring in the cost of doing business.

Advantage Number 2: Tax-Advantaged 1256 Contracts

Many options traders are unaware of the significant tax savings available when trading certain options products like index options on the S&P 500 (SPX). SPX options are considered Section 1256 contracts by the IRS and receive preferential tax treatment.

For short-term capital gains on non-1256 options and equities held less than one year, the tax rate is typically around 28%. However, with 1256 contracts like SPX options, only 40% of the gains are taxed at the higher short-term rate while 60% are taxed at the lower, long-term capital gains rate of around 15%.

The ability to get lower blended tax rates by trading options on indices like SPX can substantially improve overall profits due to tax savings. This is not well understood by many options traders.

For example, on $100,000 in short-term capital gains, the total tax bill would be $28,000 using regular options at the 28% rate, but with SPX 1256 contracts, the total tax would only be $20,200 due to the blended 20% effective rate.

This saves $8,000 in taxes on the $100,000 in gains, or an extra 8% more in net profit. Just by choosing tax-advantaged options like SPX to trade, overall returns can be meaningfully improved after tax.

Advantage Number 3: Diversification Through “Teenies”

Trading options on broad market indexes like the SPX or /ES presents a unique opportunity to diversify your portfolio and protect against market crashes through the strategic use of “teenie” options.

Firstly, index options provide inherent diversification by granting exposure to a wide array of stocks across multiple sectors. This built-in diversification helps mitigate the impact of single-stock risk and sector-specific volatility on your overall portfolio.

Secondly, and perhaps more importantly, index options allow you to leverage “teenies” - far out-of-the-money put options with Deltas around 0.03 - as a cost-effective crash insurance policy. By proactively incorporating “teenies” into your options strategy, you can position yourself to capitalize on the exponential gains that often accompany market downturns.

The power of “teenies” lies in their affordability, scalability, and potential for outsized returns during crashes. As panicked investors rush to hedge their portfolios during a market meltdown, the demand for these far out-of-the-money puts skyrockets, leading to exponential price increases. By already owning “teenies” before the crash occurs, you can turn the tables on market chaos and reap significant rewards.

Strike Prices, Widths, & Deltas:

The next crucial aspect of the All-Market Profits 1-2-2 is selecting the appropriate strike prices and widths. Here’s a breakdown of a typical position:

Middle ShortSell minus 2 Puts at negative 18 Delta

This breakdown accomplishes the following objectives:

Objective #1: Out-Of-The-Money

The entire structure is placed around 100 to 250 points outof- the-money (if trading SPX) depending on volatility levels. Placing the trade strikes a balance between returns while mitigating risk with a long and flat Gamma runway.

Objective #2: Delta Neutrality

The next objective with each new open position is to obtain Delta neutrality. If you were to add up the total Delta of the legs, the aim is to keep it as close to zero as possible.

Objective #3: Positioning & Dynamic Widths Based On Deltas

I prefer to determine the widths of the legs using “Delta- Based Selection” rather than based on fixed widths of strike prices. This means that the “width” of each leg of the structure (in terms of strike prices) will not always be the same due to volatility and underlying conditions when the trade is placed.

In a high-volatility environment, the structure would typically be further out of the money because it gives you more room for the underlying asset to move without affecting your position adversely. Higher volatility implies that the outer wings can be set wider apart and you can afford to place the structure further out of the money, capturing more premium while managing risk.

Conversely, in a lower-volatility environment, the structure would be closer to the current market price (closer to the money) to capture more premium, as there’s less expectation of drastic price movements.

Expirations

Choosing the appropriate expiration for your options structure is a balancing act between maximizing Theta decay and maintaining a sufficient runway for the trade to develop. I prefer to place my trades around 100-150 days until expiration for the following reasons:

Positive Headroom:

By selecting an expiration that is roughly 100 to 150 days out, the structure can benefit from significant Theta decay while still maintaining a long enough runway to with-stand short-term price fluctuations. This positive head-room is essential for the overall profitability of the strategy.

Flat Gamma Profile:

With a longer expiration, the Gamma profile of the structure remains relatively flat, providing a more stable Delta over time. This flat Gamma profile helps to minimize the impact of directional moves on the position’s profitability.

If you choose to go closer in time than 100 - 150 days until expira-tion, while you’ll experience more Theta decay, you will also have a shorter runway and a less flat Gamma line and, there-fore, you’ll have less downside protection. Furthermore, in order to manage this shorter runway and directional risk, you might have to make more adjustments and adjustments cost money and drawdowns could potentially be higher.

On the flip side, if I choose to go farther out in time, I’d cer-tainly have a longer runway and less risk, but the returns and Theta decay will also be lower.
